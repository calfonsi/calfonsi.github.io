Working in the Grey: Troubleshooting When Procedures Don’t Fit

This tool helps Customer Care troubleshoot confidently when issues fall outside clearly defined procedures.

What Does “Working In The Grey” Mean?

Our troubleshooting articles cover the most common issues subscribers experience. These are the black and white cases, clear steps, known errors, and documented fixes. Working in the grey means knowing what to do when the issue doesn’t fit those rules.

Sometimes a problem:

  • Isn’t caused by the BritBox app
  • Doesn’t affect all subscribers, or only BritBox subscribers, but other streaming apps as well
  • Is tied to a specific device model or setting
  • Is tied to the subscribers internet provider, setting, router or other parts of their network
  • Has already been reported by the device manufacturer

In these cases, the solution may live outside BritBox.

Working in the grey helps you resolve issues faster by reducing repeat contacts and avoiding unnecessary escalations. 

When you take time to think critically and connect the right details, you build subscriber trust and provide our tech teams with clearer, more useful context when escalation is truly needed. You won’t find every answer in our documentation—and that’s okay. 

Your critical thinking is part of the solution.

When Should You Work In The Grey?

Use critical thinking and outside research when you notice patterns like:

  • The subscriber has completed all standard troubleshooting
  • The issue happens on one device type only, and not widely reported by other subscribers
  • Perhaps the audio works, but video does not (or vice versa)
  • The issue affects specific episodes or playback, but not browsing
  • Reinstalling or updating the app makes the issue worse
  • The back and forth on the ticket is looping without new findings, this could be with the subscriber and HCL

These are signs the issue may be device-level, or related to the subscribers network, not on the app-level.

Working The Grey Basics

Start With The Facts

Before searching, collect:

  • Device brand (Roku, Samsung, LG, etc.)
  • Exact device model
  • Software / OS version
  • App version
  • What the subscriber sees (example: “green screen with sound”)

Clear details lead to better answers. Check out our BCT Data Gathering Guide article for more details on gathering data.

Use Targeted Internet Searches

Use Google to search combinations. Examples include:

  • Device model + error description
  • Device brand + playback issue
  • Green screen + sound + Roku
  • Dolby Vision + streaming issue

💡 Tip: Add words like 'forum,' 'community,' or 'known issue' to your search. More information on this topic is below.

Check Device Partner Resources

Many device partners, and other resources, publish known issues and fixes. Examples include:

  • Roku Community forums
  • Samsung support articles
  • Manufacturer help centers

If you find a match:

  • Read carefully to confirm the symptoms are the same
  • Look for comments showing the issue is widespread
  • Check whether the partner has acknowledged the issue

Ask: “Is This Inside or Outside BritBox?”

Use logic. Sample thinking includes:

  • If browsing works but playback fails → likely device or display settings
  • If subtitles and audio appear → content is loading
  • If other apps show similar issues → not a BritBox problem
  • If subscriber explains this happens on all streaming apps → might be their network
  • If extreme buffering happens on 4K content → age of device will impact performance
  • If their playback keep failing → likely device is located too far from the router

This helps you decide whether to escalate or guide the subscriber elsewhere.

Working In The Grey Is Not

Working in the grey is not about guessing or cutting corners. It doesn’t mean skipping required troubleshooting steps, sending subscribers away without real help, or escalating issues without first taking time to analyze what’s happening. 

Working in the grey means slowing down to think, doing the research, and connecting the dots between what the subscriber is experiencing and what the tools, systems, and policies are telling you. 

It’s thoughtful problem-solving, not guesswork.

Searching Smarter (Not Harder)

When documentation doesn’t fully explain an issue, an internet search can help you spot patterns, known problems, and likely causes. The goal isn’t to find a perfect answer. It’s to gather enough information to make a smart, informed decision before escalating.

Start with a clear, focused search. Use simple keywords that match what the subscriber is experiencing. Include details like:

  • The device type (TV model, phone, tablet, browser)
  • The app name (BritBox)
  • Any error message or on-screen wording
  • What the subscriber was trying to do when the issue happened

Next, check more than one type of source. Different sources can confirm whether an issue is widespread or device-specific:

  • General search engines for recent reports or outages
  • App store reviews to see if other users report the same issue
  • Manufacturer or platform support pages for known issues or device limits
  • Forums or community posts to spot recurring problems

Make sure your search matches the subscriber's region. Results can change based on location, especially for device support or known issues.

  • If you have access to a VPN, set it to the subscriber’s region before searching
  • If a VPN isn’t available, include the country or region in your search terms

This approach helps surface region-specific issues that may not appear in our documentation. Even partial answers can help you connect the dots and decide on the best next step. 

Here are some helpful steps in your research:

Step 1: Identify What Kind Of Problem This Is

Before searching, decide which category the issue fits into. Here are some examples:

This foundational thinking may help tell you what search to run. 

Step 2: Search The Device Itself (Not BritBox)

When you have the device model, use it. Example: If the subscriber reports heavy buffering and the device model looks old:

Try searches like:

  • “Samsung UN40H5003 buffering streaming”
  • “Samsung UN40H5003 release year”
  • “Samsung UN40H5003 performance streaming apps”
  • “Is Samsung UN40H5003 still supported”

What you’re looking for:

  • Year of release
  • Known performance limits
  • Complaints about slow apps or buffering
  • Mentions of outdated processors or memory

Step 3: Search By Symptom + Model

Combine what the subscriber sees with the device details.

Examples:

  • “Roku 3820 green screen with sound”
  • “LG TV app buffering after update”
  • “Sony Bravia slow streaming apps”
  • “Fire TV Stick 1st gen buffering”

A tip might be to add words like:

  • "forum"
  • "fix"
  • "community"
  • "known issue"
  • "after update"
  • "developer solution"

These often surface real subscriber experiences, solutions or fixes that can be shared, all without ever asking HCL.

Step 4: Use Trusted Public Sources

You don’t need to read everything, it helps to focus on patterns and site URL's. Make sure your researching on a website which is known or reputable. 

Common high-value sites:

  • Manufacturer community forums (Roku, Samsung, LG)
  • Reddit (device-specific threads)
  • Official support pages
  • Tech review sites (older reviews often mention performance)

Step 5: Age Matters (A Lot)

Streaming requirements change over time as apps, security standards, and video quality continue to evolve.

Devices that worked well years ago may no longer be able to keep up with modern streaming demands, even if they still power on and connect to the internet.

If the device:

  • Is over 8–10 years old
  • Has no recent OS updates
  • Struggles with other streaming apps
  • Was designed before HD / 4K became standard

Then poor performance is expected and does not point to a BritBox defect. You don’t need to prove this, you just need to recognize it and factor it into your next steps.

Step 6: Turn Research Into An Educated (Documented) Conclusion

Ask yourself:

  • Does this issue happen across many apps?
  • Is the device known to be underpowered?
  • Is the problem widely reported online?
  • Does the symptom match known hardware limits?

If yes, document that thinking clearly. Example internal note:

“Based on device model research, this TV was released in 2013. Multiple forums report buffering and slow app performance due to hardware limits. Likely device performance issue rather than BritBox app.”

Step 7: Talking To The Subscriber (Without Blaming)

When it’s likely a non-BritBox limitation:

  • Be factual, not dismissive
  • Explain streaming has changed over time
  • Offer alternatives when possible
  • Offer courtesy discounts, within the threshold limits

Check out our Discounts article for more details.

Example language:

“This TV model is over 10 years old, and many streaming apps now require more processing power than it was designed for. This can cause buffering even with good internet.”

Real-Life Example: When Searching First Would Have Helped

A subscriber reported that episodes 3 and 4 of 'Pride and Prejudice (Remastered)' would not play on their Roku Streaming Stick 4K. Audio played normally, but the video appeared as a green screen.

This issue was logged under ZD #1469089 and initially treated as a possible content or app problem. The subscriber completed all standard troubleshooting steps, including reinstalling the BritBox app and confirming they were on the latest version (4.141.2). 

After reinstalling, the issue became worse: all episodes showed a green screen with sound.

A search of previous tickets revealed ZD #1416771, which documented a known Roku device issue with the same symptoms. The root cause was not related to BritBox content or the BritBox app, but to a Roku display setting.

What A Search Revealed

The solution pointed to a Roku community thread describing a known issue with certain Roku Streaming Stick 4K models when the display type is set to Auto / 4K Dolby Vision.

Roku has acknowledged this as a device-level issue and is collecting data to fix it.

Roku Community Reference: https://community.roku.com/discussions/tv-and-players/green-screen-with-autodolby-vision-setting-on-4khdr-stick/1096235

The Fix

Changing the Roku display setting resolved the issue:

  • Go to Settings > Display type
  • Change from Auto / 4K Dolby Vision to Plain 4K (or 4K TV 60Hz)
  • Restart the Roku

Why This Matters

If a search had been done earlier:

  • The known device issue would have surfaced quickly
  • Duplicate investigation could have been avoided
  • The customer could have received a faster resolution

The Response

"Thank you so much for your patience while we investigated this further, and for sending through the photos, they were very helpful.

We’ve now confirmed that this issue is not caused by the BritBox app or the episodes themselves. What you’re seeing is a known device-level issue affecting certain Roku Streaming Stick 4K models when they are set to automatically use Dolby Vision or HDR.

Roku has acknowledged this issue and is currently collecting data to resolve it. When the device outputs video in this mode, it can result in a green screen while audio continues to play, which matches exactly what you’re experiencing.

Many other Roku customers have resolved this by changing the Roku display setting:

  • Press Home on your Roku remote
  • Go to Settings > Display type
  • Change the setting from Auto / 4K Dolby Vision to 4K TV (60Hz) or Plain 4K
  • Restart the Roku and try playing the episode again

This workaround does not affect your BritBox account or subscription, and it won’t reduce access to content.

Because this is a Roku device issue, the long-term fix will come from Roku via a system update. In the meantime, adjusting the display setting above should allow you to watch without the green screen.

We’re very sorry for the frustration this has caused and appreciate your patience while we worked to identify the root cause."

This example shows why searching past tickets and known issues is critical, even when a problem looks like a BritBox error at first glance.
