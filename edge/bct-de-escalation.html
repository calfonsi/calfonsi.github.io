De-Escalation

This article explains how to manage escalated interactions, prevent issues from intensifying, and respond with empathy and professionalism to keep subscribers feeling supported.

Introduction

In every interaction, our goal is to make sure subscribers, whether they contact us by email, messaging, or phone, finish the conversation feeling helped and supported. Before they reach out, they may already feel certain emotions that affect how the exchange goes.

Whether they are calm, frustrated, or upset, it is important to understand where they are coming from and what is behind their concerns. When a subscriber shows strong emotions that could lead to an escalation, stay calm, listen carefully, and work to bring the conversation back to a positive and respectful tone.

Identifying Escalations

Escalated interactions often happen when a subscriber feels unheard, runs into the same problem more than once, or thinks their concern is not being resolved fast enough.

In a streaming service, escalations can sound like:

  • “I’ve already contacted you three times about this and nothing is fixed!”
  • “Why does this keep happening every time I try to watch something?”
  • “I’m paying for this service. This is unacceptable!”
  • “I want to talk to a supervisor right now.”

⚠️ Attention: These signs show that emotions are rising. Recognizing them early helps agents adjust their tone, slow the conversation, and bring it back under control before frustration increases.

Recognizing Escalation vs Feedback

Not every emotional or intense message from a subscriber needs to be escalated. It is important to tell when a situation truly requires help from a higher team and when the subscriber is simply sharing feedback or frustration that can be handled in the moment.

Escalate only when the issue cannot be resolved at the frontline level or when the subscriber clearly asks to speak with a manager.

When It's An Escalation

A subscriber concern requires escalation when:

  • The subscriber asks to speak with a supervisor or manager, or directly requests that their issue be 'escalated' or 'reviewed by someone higher.'
  • The issue involves repeated failed resolutions after multiple attempts (for example, the same playback issue persists even after multiple rounds of troubleshooting.).
  • The subscriber expresses high frustration or dissatisfaction and is seeking immediate action, stating that they want to contact management, not just to vent.
  • The issue involves billing disputes, system errors, or technical problems that the frontline team cannot resolve due to limited system access and therefore requires supervisor intervention to de-escalate the situation.

Example:

  • “I’ve already contacted you three times, and no one has fixed my problem. I want to speak with a supervisor now.”
  • “I’m still being charged even though I canceled. This needs to be fixed immediately. I’m tired of speaking to agents. Please transfer me to someone else.”
  • “Your app is broken again. I’ve done everything your guide says, but it’s still not working. I want someone higher to check this.”

In these cases, the subscriber is not only expressing frustration but is also demanding action or intervention, which means the situation requires escalation. 

When It's Feedback (Not an Escalation)

A subscriber’s message is feedback when:

  • They share suggestions or experiences that sound emotional but don’t require immediate technical or account-level action.
  • They express frustration or disappointment about something outside the agent’s control, like content availability or app design, without requesting escalation.
  • They say something like, 'I just wanted to let you know' or 'You should tell your team about this.'

Example:

  • “You really need to add more British comedies. I can’t find the ones I like anymore.”
  • “Your app freezes sometimes. It’s annoying, but I just restarted it.”
  • “It’s frustrating that I can’t change my plan directly on my TV.”

Even if the subscriber sounds upset, the message is still feedback unless they’re seeking immediate action or management involvement.

💡 Tip: The quickest way to tell the difference is by asking yourself: Is the subscriber demanding a resolution or escalation right now, or are they simply sharing an opinion or observation?

⚠️ Attention: If they want intervention, escalate. If they’re sharing insight or frustration, acknowledge their experience and log it as feedback.

What to Avoid During Escalations

When an interaction starts to feel tense or close to being escalated, it is important to manage the situation carefully before the subscriber asks for a manager. During these moments, even a small misstep can make the situation worse.

To keep control and prevent further frustration, avoid the following:

  • Becoming defensive or argumentative. Never take the subscriber’s frustration personally. Focus on solving the issue, not defending your actions or the company. Phrases like 'That’s not our fault' can make subscribers feel dismissed.
  • Interrupting or talking over the subscriber. Allow them to finish explaining the issue, even if you already know the answer. Listening fully shows respect and reduces tension.
  • Using robotic or scripted empathy. Avoid copy-paste phrases such as 'We’re sorry for the inconvenience.' Instead, personalize your response: 'I’m really sorry this keeps happening — I know that’s frustrating.'
  • Matching their tone. If the subscriber sounds angry, stay calm and professional. Lowering your own tone and slowing your pace helps reset the emotional temperature of the conversation.
  • Overexplaining policy. Avoid long justifications or rule-based explanations in the middle of an emotional moment. Acknowledge their frustration first, then calmly explain next steps.
  • Passing blame. Do not point to another team, partner, or system. Instead of 'That’s HCL’s issue,' use: 'I’ll make sure this reaches the technical team who handles these cases.'
  • Overpromising outcomes. Do not guarantee fixes or timelines that are uncertain. Setting honest, realistic expectations builds more trust than making promises that cannot be kept.
  • Forgetting to follow through. If you commit to checking back or escalating the issue, be sure to do so. A lack of follow-up can turn a mild complaint into a serious escalation.

⚠️ Attention: Subscribers are usually upset about the situation, not the person helping them. Staying calm, professional, and empathetic helps rebuild trust and move the conversation toward a positive outcome.

When to Send to the Next Level

Not every case can or should be de-escalated at the frontline. Some concerns require deeper investigation, higher-level access, or leadership intervention.

Knowing when to escalate ensures the subscriber’s issue is handled efficiently and prevents repeat frustration.

Escalate the issue when:

  • The subscriber explicitly asks to speak with a supervisor or a higher support level.
  • When the subscriber is demonstrating intolerant behavior. Check out our Intolerant Behavior article for more details.
  • The subscriber has experienced the same issue multiple times despite troubleshooting and now requests management involvement.
  • The problem involves backend systems (for example, billing errors, playback failures, or content-access discrepancies) that have caused ongoing frustration.
  • The interaction includes sensitive topics such as accessibility concerns, data privacy, or security that require higher-level handling.
  • The subscriber’s tone indicates a serious loss of confidence or trust in the service.

When escalating, include a clear summary of what has been done, what the subscriber expects, and any evidence (screenshots, logs, or error messages). This helps the next level of support act quickly and keeps the experience consistent for the subscriber.

⚠️ Attention: When a subscriber requests to speak with someone higher, agents should not attempt to de-escalate but should immediately involve their supervisor for prompt intervention.

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Acknowledgment – Showing understanding or empathy toward the subscriber’s concern before offering a solution.
  • De-escalation – The process of calming an emotional or tense interaction to prevent it from requiring supervisor intervention.
  • Empathy Statement – A sincere response that recognizes the subscriber’s frustration or inconvenience (for example, 'I understand how disappointing that must feel').
  • Escalated Interaction – A situation where a subscriber expresses strong dissatisfaction, repeated frustration, or asks to speak with a supervisor or higher team.
  • Escalation – The process of transferring a concern to a higher support level or specialized department for further assistance.
  • Feedback – Comments, opinions, or suggestions shared by a subscriber that don’t require immediate action or escalation.
  • Frontline Support – The first level of customer service that assists subscribers through messaging, phone, or email and determines when escalation is needed.
  • Intervention – When a supervisor or higher-level support steps in to assist with a concern that cannot be resolved at the frontline.
  • Overpromising – Committing to a resolution or outcome that cannot be guaranteed, which can lead to further frustration or escalation.
  • Personalized Response — A tailored, genuine reply that directly addresses the subscriber’s concern instead of using a generic template.
  • Preventive Communication — A proactive approach to avoid escalation by acknowledging emotions, setting expectations, and offering next steps early.
  • Tone Management — Maintaining a calm, professional, and empathetic tone throughout the conversation, even in emotional situations.
  • Trigger Phrase — A statement that can worsen frustration, such as 'That’s not our issue' or 'You should have checked that first.'
